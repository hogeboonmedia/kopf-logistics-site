<h2><span style="font-weight: 400;">What Is a Freight Broker Commission Split?</span></h2>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Commission splits are the way a brokerage divides the profit (also called </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">gross margin</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;"> or </span><i><span style="font-weight: 400;">net revenue</span></i><span style="font-weight: 400;">) from a load between the brokerage and the Freight Agent who booked it.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">It looks like this. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">When booking a load, a Freight Agent negotiates a rate with the Shipper and a rate with the Carrier. </span><b>The difference between those two rates is the profit.</b></p>
<blockquote><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">For example: If a Shipper pays $1,500 for the load and the Carrier is paid $1,200 of that payment, the profit on that load is $300. </span></p>
<p>$1,500 &#8211; $1,200 = $300 profit</p></blockquote>
<p><b>Then that profit is split between the Freight Brokerage and the Freight Agent.</b><span style="font-weight: 400;"> The commission split decides how that profit is shared. If the split is 60/40, for instance, the Freight Agent keeps 60% of the profit and the Brokerage keeps 40%, which would look like this:</span></p>
<blockquote><p><span style="font-weight: 400;">60% of $300 = $180 (this is what the Agent earns on the load)</span></p>
<p>40% of $300 = $120 (this is what the brokerage earns on the load)</p></blockquote>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">If the commission split was 70/30, the Agent would earn $210 (instead of $180) and the brokerage would earn $90 (instead of $120). </span></p>
<p><b>So, a commission split determines how much money an Agent actually earns per load and can influence which brokerage they choose to partner with. </b></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">It is important to note that some brokerages hire Agents as W2 employees. In this case, Agents may be salaried and earn bonuses. So when looking for a brokerage to partner with, it is important to clarify if you are a W2 employee or a 1099 contractor AND clarify pay structures.  </span></p>

<h2><span style="font-weight: 400;">How Freight Broker Commissions Work</span></h2>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In any business, revenue is sales, this is the “top line” of the financial picture. When you subtract expenses you are left with profit. </span></p>
<blockquote><p><b>Revenue &#8211; Expenses = Profit</b></p></blockquote>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In the world of freight, revenue is generated from loads booked. This rate is negotiated between a Shipper and a Freight Agent and agreed upon before the load is moved from point A to point B. The expenses are typically the Carrier rate, or how much you’ll pay the trucking company or Truck Driver to transport the load. What is left after that is profit. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">As you know from reading the previous section, profit is divided between the Freight Agent and the brokerage. This is known as the commission split. Freight Agents are typically 1099 independent contractors and are paid based on commission. The percentage of the commission split Freight Brokers keep covers operational costs like insurance, technology, business-office support, and potential bad debt.   </span></p>
<p><b>An Agent’s earnings are based on a commission split of the profit. </b></p>

<h2><span style="font-weight: 400;">Common Freight Broker Commission Structures</span></h2>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Since freight brokerages and Freight Agents earn commission from the profit of a load, the brokerage and Freight Agent share that margin using a negotiated split. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Here are the most common commission structures: </span></p>
<ul>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1"><span style="font-weight: 400;">50/50 split</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1"><span style="font-weight: 400;">60/40 split</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1"><span style="font-weight: 400;">70/30 split (common for experienced agents with their own book of business)</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1"><span style="font-weight: 400;">80/20 split (less common)</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Flat-fee structures (less common)</span></li>
<li style="font-weight: 400;" aria-level="1"><span style="font-weight: 400;">Salary + small commission variations</span></li>
</ul>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">In each of these commission splits, the Freight Agent earns the larger percentage and the brokerage earns the smaller.  </span></p>
<p><b>Bottom line: splits vary depending on the brokerage. </b></p>

<h2><span style="font-weight: 400;">What Determines the Commission You Receive</span></h2>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">There are a few considerations to keep in mind when comparing commission splits. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The first factor is the brokerage. Brokerages pay for overhead, Transportation Management Software, liability and insurance costs, surety bonds, carrier onboarding, claims handling, office support, and so on.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The second factor is the Agent. Some brokerages pay a higher rate for Agents who have more experience, a large book of business, and a strong performance history. Your sales performance and load volume can influence your commission split. </span></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">The third factor is industry norms. It is common for brokerages to offer 50/50, 60/40 and 70/30 splits. These are the percentages you will see most often. </span></p>
<p><b>Brokerages consider their expense to operate the business, the Agent they are bringing on, and industry norms to calculate their commission percentage. </b></p>

<h2><span style="font-weight: 400;">Questions People Also Ask </span></h2>
<p><b>How do freight broker commission splits work?</b></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Freight brokerages and Freight Agents earn commission from the profit margin on a load. The brokerage and Freight Agent share margins using a negotiated split. This is often 50/50, 60/40, or 70/30 depending on the brokerage.</span></p>
<p><b>What is the average commission for a freight brokerage?</b></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Most Freight Brokerages earn 30–40% of the load margin depending on their role. Independent Freight Agents often earn 60–70%.</span></p>
<p><b>What is a good commission split for a Freight Agent?</b></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">A competitive split is typically 60/40 or 70/30 for Agents bringing their own customers. Newer Agents may start with a lower split if the brokerage provides leads, tools, or heavy operational support. Kopf offers a competitive 70/30 commission split to Independent Freight Agents with experience, a strong performance history and a solid book of business.</span></p>
<p><b>How do Freight Agents get paid?</b></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Freight Agents are almost always paid per load. Some brokerages offer Freight Agents weekly or biweekly payout cycles depending on their internal accounting systems. At Kopf, we offer weekly payouts to our Independent Freight Agents. </span></p>
<p><b>Why do commission splits vary between brokerages?</b></p>
<p><span style="font-weight: 400;">Splits differ based on overhead, support levels, customer ownership, business-office services, technology costs, and whether the brokerage provides leads or requires the Agent to build their own book of business.</span></p>
